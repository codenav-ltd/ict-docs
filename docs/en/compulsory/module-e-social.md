# Module E · Social Implications

> **Suggested time:** 8 hours · **The smallest module but highest marks-per-hour.** Easy ethics arguments earn structured-question marks.

This module asks you to think about how ICT affects society — not just whether it is "good" or "bad", but the specific economic, legal, social, ethical and security consequences.

## a. Technological Innovations (3h)

### Learning outcomes

- Understand the basic concepts of **technological innovations** and their applications.

### Innovations on the syllabus

| Innovation | Real-life examples |
|------------|---------------------|
| **Artificial Intelligence (AI)** | Voice assistants (Siri, Google), recommendation systems, ChatGPT, image generation, autonomous cars |
| **Data Science** | Big data analytics in banking, health, retail, government smart-city projects |
| **3D Printing** | Rapid prototyping, custom prosthetics, dental moulds |
| **Augmented Reality (AR)** | Pokémon GO, AR makeup try-on apps, AR navigation overlays |
| **Virtual Reality (VR)** | Gaming, training simulators (pilots, surgeons), virtual museums |

### Talking points

- **Pattern recognition** is the core building block of modern AI — image recognition, speech recognition, fraud detection.
- 3D printing reduces lead time and cost for low-volume manufacturing.
- AR overlays digital content on the physical world; VR replaces the physical world entirely.

## b. Health & Ethical Issues (3h)

### Learning outcomes

- Identify **health hazards** associated with ICT and propose **ergonomic** practices.
- Realise the importance of **equity of access**.
- Discuss **ethical considerations** on the use of ICT.

### Common health hazards

| Hazard | Cause | Prevention |
|--------|-------|------------|
| Repetitive Strain Injury (RSI), Carpal Tunnel | Repeated wrist/finger motion | Ergonomic keyboard, breaks, stretching |
| Eye strain, dry eye, computer vision syndrome | Long screen time, blue light | 20-20-20 rule, brightness/contrast |
| Back/neck pain | Poor posture | Chair height, monitor at eye level |
| Hearing damage | Loud headphone use | Volume limits, breaks |
| Sleep disorders | Late-night screen use | Night mode, screen-free hour before bed |
| Mental health | Social media overuse | Usage limits, digital detox |

### Ergonomics tips

- Screen at arm's length, top of screen at eye level
- Chair supports lower back; feet flat on floor or footrest
- Wrists straight when typing; keyboard at elbow height
- Break every 30 minutes (20-20-20: every 20 min look 20 ft away for 20 sec)

### Equity of access ("digital divide")

| Dimension | Issue |
|-----------|-------|
| Income | Cost of computers and broadband |
| Geography | Rural areas with poor coverage |
| Age | Older users less familiar with digital interfaces |
| Disability | Inaccessible websites and apps |
| Gender | Historical under-representation in tech careers |
| Language | Most online content is in English |

### Ethical considerations

- **Information freedom** — pros: democratisation of knowledge; cons: spread of misinformation, hate speech.
- **Privacy** — should companies track everything you do online?
- **Bias in AI** — algorithms trained on biased data perpetuate discrimination.
- **Autonomous weapons** and other dual-use technology.

## c. Intellectual Property (2h)

### Learning outcomes

- Understand the basic ideas of **intellectual property** and **copyright**.
- Understand the benefits/risks of licensing schemes — **freeware, shareware, open source, copyrighted**.
- Relate acts of possible **infringement** in software and Internet piracy.
- Be aware of ways to **reduce IP theft** on digital property.
- Know some **legal consequences** in Hong Kong, especially in education.

### Licensing schemes

| Scheme | Cost | Source code | Example | Notes |
|--------|------|-------------|---------|-------|
| **Copyrighted (commercial)** | Paid | Closed | Microsoft Office, Adobe Photoshop | Pay to use, redistribution forbidden |
| **Shareware** | Free trial, then paid | Closed | WinRAR | "Try before you buy" |
| **Freeware** | Free | Closed | Some Microsoft tools | Free but proprietary |
| **Open Source (FOSS)** | Free | Open | Linux, Firefox, Python, MySQL | Free, may modify and share |

### Acts of infringement

- Downloading software, music, movies without paying
- Photocopying or scanning a copyrighted book
- Sharing course materials online without permission
- Selling counterfeit DVDs / USBs with pirated software
- Using stock images on a website without licence

### Anti-piracy measures

- **Digital Rights Management (DRM)**
- **Digital watermarks** (visible or invisible)
- **Digital signatures** to prove authenticity
- Activation codes / online licence checks
- Education on respect for IP

### Hong Kong legal context

- **Copyright Ordinance (Cap. 528)** governs copyright in Hong Kong.
- Criminal liability for **commercial-scale** infringement.
- Schools and universities have specific provisions for fair dealing in education.
- "End user piracy" — using unlicensed software at work or school — can lead to fines or imprisonment.

## Common mistakes

- Calling all free software "open source" — freeware is free but **not** open source.
- Believing "I bought it, I own the copyright" — buying a copy does not transfer the copyright.
- Treating AR and VR as the same thing.
- Forgetting to mention **specific groups** when discussing the digital divide (rural elderly, persons with disabilities, etc.).

## How to score on ethics questions

1. **State the issue clearly** (one sentence).
2. **Give 2 specific examples** — concrete > generic.
3. **Explain who is affected and how**.
4. **Suggest a balanced response** — both individual and government / industry actions.

## Past-paper hotspots

- MC: identify whether a given scenario is fair use, infringement, or neutral.
- Section B: discuss the ethical implications of a new technology in 4–6 marks.
- Section B: ergonomic recommendations for a given workspace photo.
